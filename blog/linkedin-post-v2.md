I read everything about AI for months and still couldn't explain how any of it fit together.

Not because I didn't understand the pieces. Agents, context engineering, business model shifts — each article made sense on its own. But when I tried to see the big picture — how agents connect to business shifts, how context relates to verification — the synthesis just wouldn't come.

The more I read, the fuzzier it got.

Charlie Munger had the fix: "If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form."

So I stopped consuming and started structuring. I compressed everything into 5 Big Ideas, each with an everyday analogy:

1. The Kitchen, Not the Stove — context is the product, not the model
2. The Race Track, Not the Race Car — the harness matters more than AI intelligence
3. A Team, Not an Employee — how agents work (and why non-technical people have an edge)
4. Agents Are the New Customers — business models are inverting
5. The Snowball — knowledge only compounds if you process it

The surprising part? I didn't learn anything new. Every concept was already in my notes. What was missing was structure — 5 shelves that turned a pile of fragments into a framework.

There's so much happening in AI right now — agents, context engineering, business models inverting, new tools every week — and honestly, I've been struggling to put it all together in my head.

It's not that I don't understand any of it. Each piece makes sense when I read about it. But seeing how everything connects into one coherent picture? That's been genuinely hard.

I spent months collecting and cross-linking AI insights, and at some point I realized: the problem isn't that I need to read more. It's that I haven't processed what I already have.

Charlie Munger put it well: "If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form."

So I tried something different. Instead of reading article number 125, I stopped and asked: what are the 5 big ideas that everything I've been reading is really about?

Here's what I landed on:

1. The Kitchen, Not the Stove — context is the product, not the model
2. The Race Track, Not the Race Car — the harness matters more than AI intelligence
3. A Team, Not an Employee — how agents work (and why non-technical people have an edge)
4. Agents Are the New Customers — business models are inverting
5. The Snowball — knowledge only compounds if you process it

Each one uses an everyday analogy to make the jargon click. If you've been feeling overwhelmed by the pace of AI, this might resonate.

AI is changing how software gets built, who buys it, what skills matter, and which businesses survive — all at the same time.

If you've been trying to keep up, you know the feeling: every article makes sense on its own. But stepping back to see how it all connects? The big picture somehow gets fuzzier the more you read.

I spent months building a knowledge graph of AI insights — extracting and cross-linking everything I consumed. I had over a hundred interconnected concepts. And I still couldn't explain to someone how agents reshaping software connects to business models inverting connects to context mattering more than model intelligence.

The problem wasn't knowledge. It was synthesis.

Charlie Munger solved this decades ago: "If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form."

So I stopped reading and started structuring. I compressed everything into 5 Big Ideas — each with an everyday analogy that makes the jargon click:

1. The Kitchen, Not the Stove — why context is the product, not the model
2. The Race Track, Not the Race Car — why the harness matters more than AI intelligence
3. A Team, Not an Employee — how agents actually work (and why non-technical people have an edge)
4. Agents Are the New Customers — how business models are inverting
5. The Snowball — why knowledge only compounds if you process it

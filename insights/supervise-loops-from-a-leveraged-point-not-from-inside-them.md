Zhao challenges the reflexive "human-in-the-loop" virtue: programming agents "taught us that having a 'human-in-the-loop' isn't always desirable. It's like having someone personally inspect every bolt on a factory line, or walk in front of a car to clear the road" — a reference to Britain's 1865 Red Flag Act, which required a flag bearer to walk ahead of every motor vehicle. "We want humans to supervise the loops from a leveraged point, not be in them."

This sharpens the management shift in [[parallel-agents-create-management-problem-not-coding-problem]]: the win isn't reviewing each agent's every step, it's positioning yourself to oversee many loops at once — the "manager of infinite minds" stance behind [[treat-ai-like-distributed-team-not-assistant]], operationalized by an [[orchestrator-agent-replaces-human-coordination]]. The unsolved precondition is verifiability — Zhao concedes humans must stay in the loop for now precisely because general knowledge work can't be auto-verified the way code can, which is why [[production-agents-route-decisions-not-every-call-to-llm]] reserves human attention for genuine ambiguity rather than spending it on every routine pass.
